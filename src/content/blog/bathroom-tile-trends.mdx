Tile is the single material decision with the most visual impact in any bathroom renovation. It sets the tone, establishes the palette, and more than anything else determines whether your bathroom feels like a spa, a classic retreat, or a dated afterthought.

I install tile in bathrooms throughout Shakopee, Eden Prairie, Chanhassen, Prior Lake, and the broader Twin Cities area, and I track what's actually working  -  what looks timeless when finished, what grows on you, and what clients occasionally wish they'd reconsidered. Here's what I'm seeing and thinking about heading into 2025 and beyond.

## The Big Picture: Where Tile Design Is Going

The defining shift in bathroom tile right now is a move away from uniformity toward texture, personality, and warmth. The perfectly smooth, perfectly white, perfectly consistent tile aesthetic that defined the 2010s is giving way to something with more character  -  handmade variation, artisan surface quality, earthy tones, and unexpected shapes.

At the same time, large-format tile continues to grow in popularity because of its clean, sophisticated look and practical ease of cleaning. Both trends coexist, often beautifully, in the same bathroom.

## Trends Worth Serious Consideration

### Zellige Tile

Zellige (pronounced "zehl-eej") is a Moroccan handmade ceramic tile with an irregular, faceted surface and slightly uneven glaze. Each piece is unique. The imperfection is the point  -  and the result, particularly in a light, reflective tone like white or soft ivory, is extraordinary.

The way zellige catches and reflects light  -  with subtle shimmer and variation rather than flat uniformity  -  creates a bathroom wall or shower that looks different at different times of day and in different lighting conditions. It's genuinely alive in a way that standard subway tile isn't.

**Best applications:** Shower walls, the accent wall behind a freestanding tub, powder room walls where a small amount creates maximum impact.

**Honest considerations:** Zellige is more expensive than standard tile ($15–$45+ per square foot for quality product) and more labor-intensive to install because of its irregular sizing. Grout lines will be visible and slightly uneven  -  this is correct and intentional, not a flaw. The right contractor (one who has installed zellige before) makes a significant difference in the result.

### Large-Format Tile

Nothing says clean, modern luxury like large-format porcelain tile. 12x24 is the new standard; 24x24 and 24x48 slabs are increasingly common in upscale renovations. Fewer grout lines means a smoother visual surface, easier cleaning, and a sense of openness that smaller tile doesn't achieve.

The stone-look porcelains available today  -  from Calacatta marble looks to warm quartzite-inspired designs  -  are genuinely beautiful, and many are indistinguishable from natural stone at a glance.

**Best applications:** Shower floors and walls, bathroom floors, feature walls.

**Honest considerations:** Large tiles are unforgiving on uneven subfloors. Installation requires careful substrate preparation, and the tile itself is heavier  -  important for floor load considerations and for wall installation in showers. A skilled tile setter is non-negotiable for large-format tile work.

### Warm-Toned Neutrals

The shift from cool grays and stark whites toward warmer tones is one of the clearest and most durable trends in tile right now. Creamy whites, warm taupes, soft terracottas, and earthy greiges are replacing cool platinum and ice white as the dominant neutrals.

These warm tones photograph well, age gracefully, and feel genuinely comfortable to spend time around  -  which matters in a room you start and end your day in.

A warm white tile (think a creamy white with undertones of sand or blush rather than cool blue-gray) pairs beautifully with natural wood accents, warm-toned hardware, and the organic shapes of modern bathroom design.

### Graphic and Patterned Floor Tile

The bathroom floor is a small canvas where a bold statement can read as sophisticated rather than overwhelming. Graphic patterned floor tile  -  encaustic cement tiles, Moroccan patterns, graphic black-and-white geometric patterns, or handpainted majolica  -  creates personality and visual interest within a small footprint.

The design principle that works well: keep walls and shower relatively simple and let the floor do the talking. A white-tiled shower with a stunning patterned floor is a classic combination that always looks curated and intentional.

**Cement tiles require sealing** and more maintenance than porcelain. If you love the look but want less maintenance, many porcelain manufacturers now make excellent replicas of cement tile patterns that are durable and easy to care for.

### Fluted and Textured Tile

Fluted tile  -  with vertical ridges that create a corrugated or ribbed surface  -  has become a genuinely popular design element in bathrooms. It adds architectural texture and visual interest to a vanity surround, a niche interior, or a shower wall accent without requiring a complex tile pattern.

The three-dimensional quality catches light in an interesting way and creates shadow and depth that flat tile doesn't. Fluted tile in a warm white or matte stone tone is a particularly popular look right now.

### Terrazzo

Terrazzo  -  composite material made with chips of marble, quartz, or granite set in a binder, then polished  -  has made a strong comeback from its mid-century origins. Modern terrazzo tile is beautiful, durable, and unique, with a scattered-chip pattern that's distinctive and warm.

It works particularly well for bathroom floors, where its durability and visual richness create a statement. Terrazzo pairs naturally with warm wood vanities, white walls, and organic shapes.

## What I'd Think Carefully About

### Very Bold Tile Colors for Major Surfaces

Deep hunter green, cobalt blue, or terracotta orange tile can look absolutely stunning in the right design. But tile is the element of a bathroom that's hardest and most expensive to change. If you're considering a bold color for a large surface  -  the shower walls, the entire floor  -  I'd encourage you to live with a sample for a few weeks and ask yourself honestly how you'll feel about it in seven or ten years.

One approach that works well: bold color on a smaller accent surface (a single niche, the back wall of a shower, the powder room walls) keeps the drama while limiting commitment. Your full bathrooms stay fresher longer.

### Matching Everything

Bathrooms where every element is the exact same tile  -  floor, shower floor, shower walls, accent wall  -  can feel monotonous rather than cohesive. Mixing tile thoughtfully (a floor tile that complements but contrasts slightly with the shower wall tile, for example) creates visual interest and makes the design feel considered.

A good rule of thumb: two or three tile types per bathroom is often better than one. But make sure they share something  -  a tone family, a material quality, a complementary color.

## Tile Installation: Why Skill Matters So Much

The most beautiful tile looks bad if it's installed poorly. Uneven grout lines, tiles that aren't level, corners that don't align  -  these are things you see every time you walk into the room.

Tile installation is one of the trades where the quality differential between an average installer and a skilled one is very visible in the finished product. When you're investing in quality tile, the installation matters as much as the material selection.

Questions worth asking when vetting a tile installer:
- Can I see photos of recent projects similar to what I'm planning?
- Have you installed this specific tile type before (large-format, zellige, cement tile)?
- What substrate preparation do you do before setting tile?
- How do you handle grout line consistency in large-format tile work?

## What to Budget for Tile Work

Here's a realistic range for tile installation in our market:

- **Standard ceramic/porcelain floor or wall tile:** $12–$22 per square foot installed
- **Large-format tile (12x24 and above):** $18–$30 per square foot installed
- **Mosaic tile:** $20–$35 per square foot installed
- **Specialty tile (zellige, cement):** $25–$50+ per square foot installed

These ranges are for tile and installation. Design tiles like zellige or imported cement patterns will add significantly to the material cost.
