"How much does a handyman cost?" is one of the most common questions I hear from homeowners in Shakopee, Burnsville, and the surrounding suburbs  -  and it's one that deserves a real answer rather than a vague "it depends."

The truth is, there's a wide range of what people call a "handyman" and what they charge. In this post, I want to give you honest, current numbers for our market, help you understand what you're actually paying for, and clarify when a handyman is the right call versus when you need a licensed contractor.

## What Is a Handyman, Exactly?

In Minnesota, there's no specific license for "handyman." The term generally refers to someone who handles a range of smaller home repair and maintenance tasks  -  things like fixing doors that don't latch, patching drywall, replacing light fixtures, caulking, assembling furniture, hanging items, minor plumbing fixture replacements, and general maintenance tasks.

A licensed general contractor (like Hasselquist Contracting  -  my license is BC808643) can do all of that, plus take on larger remodeling projects, pull permits, and coordinate structural and systems work.

The line between "handyman work" and "contractor work" matters for larger projects  -  more on that in a moment.

## Current Handyman Rates in the Twin Cities (2025)

Here's what the market looks like right now in the Twin Cities metro, including Shakopee, Eden Prairie, Apple Valley, Lakeville, Eagan, and surrounding areas:

### Hourly Rates

- **Entry-level / less experienced:** $50–$75/hour
- **Experienced, general handyman:** $75–$110/hour
- **Licensed contractor doing handyman-scale work:** $95–$130/hour

Most reputable handymen and small contractors in our area have a minimum charge  -  typically 2 hours or a flat service-call fee of $100–$200. This covers travel time, showing up, and the overhead of running a business.

### Day Rates

For full-day work (8 hours), you'll often see rates of:
- $400–$600 for an entry-level handyman
- $600–$900 for experienced work
- $750–$1,000+ for a licensed contractor

If you have multiple tasks to tackle in a day, booking a full day often gives you more flexibility than paying by the hour and watching the clock.

## Why Are Rates So Different?

When you get quotes ranging from $60/hour to $120/hour, you're often paying for different things:

**Experience and expertise:** Someone who has been doing this work for 15 years moves faster, does cleaner work, and solves problems you didn't know you had. The $60/hour person who takes 4 hours on a job that a $100/hour person finishes in 90 minutes is not cheaper.

**Licensing and insurance:** A licensed, insured contractor costs more to be than an unlicensed handyman. That cost gets passed to you  -  but it also means you have legal recourse if something goes wrong, and that your homeowner's insurance will actually cover claims related to work done on your property.

**Overhead and professionalism:** A handyman running a real business  -  with a van stocked with tools and materials, a booking system, insurance, and a reputation to protect  -  has real overhead. That's not a bad thing. It's what allows them to show up reliably, warranty their work, and be there if something needs to be corrected.

**Materials markup:** Most contractors mark up materials by 10–25%. This is standard and fair  -  they've sourced, transported, and take responsibility for those materials. Be wary of any contractor who claims to pass all materials at cost with no markup; it usually means they're making it up elsewhere.

## When a Handyman Is the Right Choice

For the following types of work, a handyman is typically perfectly appropriate:

- Minor drywall repairs
- Door and window adjustments
- Replacing fixtures (faucets, light fixtures, toilets) like-for-like
- Caulking and weatherproofing
- Cabinet hardware replacement
- Painting touch-ups
- General maintenance and small repairs
- Hanging pictures, mirrors, shelving

These tasks don't typically require permits, don't involve structural or systems changes, and are within the competency of a skilled, experienced handyman.

## When You Need a Licensed Contractor Instead

Minnesota law and good judgment require a licensed contractor for:

**Anything requiring a permit:** Structural changes, electrical panel work, new circuit wiring, significant plumbing changes, HVAC modifications, and any new construction or significant remodeling work require permits  -  and permits require a licensed contractor.

**Electrical work beyond fixture replacement:** Replacing an existing light fixture with a similar one is within handyman territory. Running new circuits, upgrading panels, adding outlets, or any work that requires opening up walls and modifying the wiring system requires a licensed electrician or a licensed general contractor coordinating one.

**Plumbing beyond fixture replacement:** Replacing a faucet or toilet is fine. Moving drain lines, adding plumbing, working on supply pipes in the walls, or any work requiring a permit needs a licensed plumber or licensed GC.

**Any project over a certain dollar threshold:** Minnesota statute generally requires a residential building contractor license for projects over certain thresholds. A licensed contractor can advise you on where your project falls.

**When there's any uncertainty about what's behind the wall:** If there's potential for asbestos, lead paint, moisture damage, or unknown wiring/plumbing, a licensed contractor is the safer choice.

## The Cost of Getting It Wrong

One of the most expensive things you can do is hire a cheap, unlicensed handyman for a project that required permits and proper expertise, then discover the problem when you try to sell your home. Unpermitted electrical work, improperly installed plumbing, or structural changes done without engineering review can require costly remediation at sale  -  or can reduce your home's value or kill a deal entirely.

Homeowners in Shakopee, Prior Lake, Chanhassen, and throughout the Twin Cities metro have told me versions of this story more times than I can count. The $40/hour "savings" became a $15,000 problem.

## Tips for Hiring a Handyman or Contractor in Our Area

- **Ask for licensing and insurance** upfront, before any work begins
- **Get a written quote** for project-based work (not just hourly), even if it's a simple repair
- **Check reviews** on Google  -  patterns matter more than star counts
- **Clarify what's included** in the quote: labor only, or labor and materials?
- **Ask about the warranty** on the work
- **Understand the payment terms**  -  a small deposit on larger jobs is reasonable; paying in full before work begins is not
