I want to talk about something that sounds like a dry legal formality but is actually one of the most important decisions you'll make when hiring someone to work on your home: whether that person is licensed.

I'm a licensed residential building contractor in Minnesota — my license number is BC808643, and it's verifiable on the Department of Labor and Industry website. I'm not telling you that to pat myself on the back. I'm telling you because I want you to understand what that license actually means for you as a homeowner, and what you're risking when you hire someone without one.

## What Minnesota's Contractor Licensing Actually Requires

Minnesota's residential contractor licensing isn't just a piece of paper. To hold a valid residential building contractor license in our state, a contractor must:

- **Pass a written examination** covering residential construction, building codes, and contractor law
- **Carry general liability insurance** — currently a minimum of $100,000 per occurrence for residential contractors
- **Carry workers' compensation insurance** (or meet an exemption for sole proprietors with no employees)
- **Provide a surety bond** that protects homeowners in certain situations
- **Complete continuing education** to maintain their license
- **Be subject to regulation and discipline** by the Department of Labor and Industry

This matters because each of these requirements exists to protect you. Let's go through them.

## The Insurance Question: What's Really at Stake

### General Liability Insurance

If your contractor accidentally causes damage to your property — a plumbing connection that fails and floods your basement, a structural error that causes cracking, a fire from an electrical mistake — general liability insurance is how that damage gets paid for.

Without it, you're filing a claim against your homeowner's insurance for damage caused by someone else. Your rates may go up. You may face a deductible. And if the damage is related to work that wasn't permitted (more on that below), your claim may be denied entirely.

Licensed contractors must carry liability insurance. An unlicensed contractor almost certainly doesn't.

### Workers' Compensation Insurance

If a worker is injured on your property and the contractor doesn't have workers' compensation insurance, you — as the property owner — may be legally liable for those medical costs and lost wages. This isn't a hypothetical: it has happened to homeowners throughout the Twin Cities who hired unlicensed workers, thinking they were saving money.

Roofing, framing, demolition — construction work has genuine injury risk. Workers' comp exists to protect workers who get hurt and to protect you from being financially responsible for those injuries.

A licensed contractor carries workers' compensation or meets the requirements for a legal exemption. An unlicensed contractor almost never does.

## The Permit System: Why It Exists and What It Protects

This is the part where I need to be direct with you: if a contractor suggests skipping permits to save time or money, that should end the conversation.

Permits and inspections exist to protect the people who live in homes from unsafe construction. An inspector who catches an improperly installed electrical panel, a structural beam that's undersized for its load, or a bathroom exhaust fan that's venting into the attic isn't creating bureaucratic obstacles — they're catching problems that could cause fires, structural failures, or health hazards.

In Minnesota, major work in categories including electrical, plumbing, structural, and HVAC requires permits. This work must be done by or under the supervision of a licensed contractor. An unlicensed person cannot legally pull these permits — which means if they're doing the work anyway, it's uninspected and legally unpermitted.

### What Unpermitted Work Does to Your Home's Value

Unpermitted work creates a legal and financial problem that typically surfaces when you try to sell your home. A buyer's inspector will often flag work that appears to have been done without permits. Your real estate disclosure requires you to disclose known unpermitted work. Lenders and their appraisers can flag unpermitted additions or modifications.

In the best case, you'll be required to disclose the work and negotiate on price. In worse cases, you may need to have the work torn open and reinspected — at your expense — to demonstrate that it meets code. And if the work doesn't meet code, you pay to bring it into compliance.

The money saved by skipping permits rarely comes close to the cost of these remediation scenarios.

### Insurance and Unpermitted Work

Your homeowner's insurance policy likely contains language that excludes claims related to unpermitted construction. If an unpermitted electrical change causes a fire, you may find your claim significantly complicated or denied.

This is not hypothetical. Insurance companies investigate construction-related claims and review permit history.

## The Competency Question

Licensing requires passing an examination. This doesn't guarantee excellence — not every licensed contractor is a great contractor — but it does provide a baseline of knowledge that unlicensed contractors have not demonstrated.

More importantly, the licensing system creates accountability. A licensed contractor can have their license suspended or revoked for dishonest business practices, repeated code violations, or failure to complete work. The DLI publishes disciplinary actions. An unlicensed contractor has no such accountability framework.

When you have a problem with a licensed contractor and can't resolve it directly, you have options: the DLI's complaint process, the contractor's bond, and civil recourse against a business with real legal standing. With an unlicensed contractor, your recourse is often limited to small claims court against an individual who may have no assets and can simply stop answering your calls.

## The Price Differential — And What It Actually Represents

Unlicensed contractors typically charge less than licensed ones. This is often cited as the justification for hiring them. But it's worth understanding why they charge less:

- They don't carry insurance (no insurance premium)
- They don't pay workers' compensation (significant cost savings)
- They don't carry a bond
- They don't pay for continuing education
- They don't have the liability costs of a legitimate business

All of these "savings" are costs and risks that are transferred directly to you. The lower price is not a discount — it's a shifting of costs from the contractor to the homeowner.

A properly licensed, insured contractor builds all of these costs into their pricing. That's why they cost more. It's also why you're actually protected when you hire them.

## How to Verify a Contractor's License

In Minnesota, contractor licenses are public record and verifiable online at the Department of Labor and Industry website (dli.mn.gov).

When you search a license, you can see:
- Whether the license is active
- The license type and expiration date
- Whether any disciplinary actions have been taken

This search takes about two minutes. I'd encourage you to do it for every contractor you're seriously considering. Any reputable contractor will not only be comfortable with you looking them up but will proactively provide their license number.
