Most homes in Shakopee, Prior Lake, and the surrounding Twin Cities suburbs have at least one small bathroom  -  whether it's a 5x8 hall bath shared by the kids, a powder room off the entryway, or a secondary guest bath that hasn't been touched since the previous owners. These spaces often get ignored during renovations because homeowners assume there's not much you can do with a small footprint.

That assumption is wrong.

Some of the most striking bathrooms I've renovated have been small ones. The constraints actually force intentional design decisions that make the final result feel more curated and luxurious than a larger bathroom that was designed with less attention. Here's how to do it right.

## Rethink the Layout Before You Touch Anything Else

In small bathrooms, the layout is everything. Moving a toilet 18 inches, swapping a tub for a walk-in shower, or changing which wall the vanity sits on can dramatically change how the room feels to use.

Before committing to your renovation plan, spend some time thinking about whether the current layout actually works. A few questions worth asking:

- Does the door swing into the room and hit something? A pocket door or a barn-style door on the outside frees up significant floor space.
- Is there a bathtub that nobody uses? Replacing a standard alcove tub with a walk-in shower is probably the single highest-impact change you can make in a small bathroom. You get a shower that feels genuinely spacious, is easier to clean, and makes the room look and feel much larger.
- Is the vanity blocking natural light? Sometimes moving the vanity to an adjacent wall opens up window light significantly.

You don't need a large footprint to make this room feel special  -  you just need the layout to work.

## The Walk-In Shower Upgrade

I want to spend more time on this because it really is transformative. A 36x36 shower in the corner of a small bathroom with a glass door feels cramped and dated. Replace the same footprint with a fully tiled, doorless (or frameless glass) walk-in shower, and the room is unrecognizable.

**Curbless showers**  -  where there's no step up into the shower, just a continuous floor  -  are the gold standard. They make the visual space feel continuous and uninterrupted. They also make cleaning dramatically easier and satisfy aging-in-place accessibility needs.

**Large-format tile in the shower** makes it feel bigger, not smaller. I know this sounds counterintuitive, but large tiles have fewer grout lines, which means fewer visual interruptions, which means the eye reads the space as larger.

**A niche built into the shower wall** for shampoo and soap keeps the shower looking clean and intentional rather than cluttered with caddies hanging off the showerhead.

**A rain showerhead** transforms the shower experience. Combined with a hand wand on a slide bar, this is a $400–$700 material upgrade that feels like a luxury spa every single morning.

## Vanity Choices That Make Small Bathrooms Shine

### Float It

A wall-mounted floating vanity is the easiest way to make a small bathroom feel larger. By leaving the floor visible underneath, the eye reads the space as more open. This works in any size bathroom but is especially powerful in small ones.

The functional bonus: cleaning the floor is actually possible instead of requiring you to work around a pedestal base.

### Go Smaller and Better

In a small bathroom, you can't have a massive vanity. But you can have a well-designed, high-quality smaller one. A 24-inch or 30-inch floating vanity with a nice stone or quartz top, undermount sink, and quality fixtures often looks more sophisticated than a larger but less considered vanity.

### Mirrors Can Do Heavy Lifting

A large mirror  -  especially one that extends all the way to the ceiling  -  bounces light around the room and creates a visual sense of depth that makes small spaces feel much larger. If you have the wall space, an oversized mirror (larger than just above the vanity) is a high-impact, relatively affordable upgrade.

Alternatively, a backlit mirror or a medicine cabinet with integrated LED lighting serves double duty: storage and excellent task lighting.

## Tile Strategies for Small Bathrooms

### Extend the Tile Vertically

Taking wall tile all the way to the ceiling  -  rather than stopping at the standard 6-foot height  -  draws the eye up and makes the room feel taller. This is especially effective with large-format tile in a vertical stack or herringbone pattern.

### Keep the Color Palette Simple

In a small bathroom, a complex mix of patterns and colors tends to feel visually chaotic and closing. A simple, cohesive palette  -  perhaps two complementary materials  -  creates calm and makes the space feel more intentional.

This doesn't mean everything has to be white or neutral. A small bathroom in a rich slate blue with white fixtures and chrome hardware looks stunning and feels luxurious. The key is simplicity and cohesion, not necessarily lightness.

### Use the Same Tile on the Floor and Shower Walls

Running the same tile from the floor up into the shower (especially in a wet-room style design) creates visual continuity that makes a small space feel unified and intentional. This is a design technique used in very high-end projects that works equally well in modest-budget renovations.

## Lighting: The Most Underrated Element

Poor lighting makes every bathroom feel smaller and more dated. Great lighting makes a small bathroom feel like a boutique hotel.

### Layer Your Light Sources

**Ambient lighting:** Recessed lighting in the ceiling provides overall room illumination. Even one well-placed 4-inch recessed fixture on a dimmer is better than a single ceiling-mounted fixture.

**Task lighting:** Side-lit mirrors (sconces on either side of the mirror, or a backlit mirror with edge lighting) provide even, flattering light for your face without the harsh shadows that come from overhead-only lighting. This is the gold standard for bathroom vanity lighting.

**Accent or mood lighting:** A small LED strip under a floating vanity, a backlit mirror, or a warm-tone light in the shower niche creates ambiance and makes the room feel finished.

### Go Warm

Color temperature matters more in bathrooms than almost anywhere else, because you're looking at your face in this light every day. Warm white (2700K–3000K) is much more flattering than cool or daylight-temperature bulbs. And dimming the lights in the evening genuinely transforms a bathroom into a calming retreat.

## Hardware and Fixtures: Where Small Details Pay Off

In a small bathroom, the hardware and fixtures are a larger fraction of what you see  -  so they deserve more attention, not less. A beautiful faucet, a matte black towel bar with clean lines, and a sculptural toilet paper holder make a small bathroom feel high-end in a way that's completely disproportionate to their cost.

You don't need to spend a fortune, but you do need to be intentional. Choose a single finish and be consistent throughout: all chrome, all matte black, all brushed gold. The coherence reads as quality.

## Storage Solutions for Small Bathrooms

Storage is the most consistent challenge in small bathrooms. A few strategies that work well:

**Built-in medicine cabinet:** A recessed medicine cabinet gains you 4–5 inches of storage depth without protruding into the room. Modern medicine cabinets with integrated LED lighting are genuinely beautiful.

**Toilet tank storage:** The wall above the toilet is often wasted space. A floating shelf or built-in cabinet above the toilet is an easy, unobtrusive storage addition.

**Recessed niche in the shower:** This is essentially mandatory in any shower renovation. It holds your daily shower products neatly without caddies or bottles lined up on the ledge.

**Drawer base vanities:** A vanity with drawers rather than doors provides much more usable storage per square foot. The drawer organizers that you can add inside the drawers are a small touch that makes daily life much easier.

## What Does a Small Bathroom Renovation Cost?

For a complete renovation of a standard 5x8 or 5x9 bathroom  -  new tile, new shower, new vanity, new toilet, new fixtures, new lighting  -  you're typically looking at $10,000–$20,000 in our market, depending on material selections and whether any layout changes are involved.

That feels like significant money for a small room, but consider: this is a space you use multiple times every day, and a beautiful, functional small bathroom is one of the most noticeable improvements you can make to your home's daily livability.
