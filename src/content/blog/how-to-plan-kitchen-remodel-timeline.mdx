If you've done any research into kitchen remodeling, you've probably come across wildly different timeline estimates — everything from "six weeks" to "six months" — and you're left wondering which is closer to reality. The honest answer is: both, depending on the project.

But here's what nobody tells you often enough: a lot of the timeline for a kitchen remodel is in the planning phase, not the construction phase. The weeks of decisions, selections, and coordination that happen before a single cabinet is hung are where projects either run smoothly or start going sideways.

I've remodeled kitchens across Shakopee, Eden Prairie, Chanhassen, Prior Lake, and throughout the Twin Cities suburbs for years. Let me walk you through a realistic timeline and help you understand where the time actually goes.

## Phase 1: The Planning and Design Phase (4–10 Weeks)

This is the phase most homeowners underestimate, and it's the phase where rushing creates the most problems down the road.

### Week 1–2: Initial Consultation and Scope Definition

The process starts with a conversation about what you want and what's possible. A good contractor will visit your home, assess the existing kitchen, understand your must-haves and budget, and begin putting together a preliminary scope of work.

At this stage, you should be thinking about:
- **Big picture changes:** Are you keeping the same layout, or are you moving the sink, range, or refrigerator? Opening walls?
- **Cabinet direction:** Stock, semi-custom, or custom? This decision affects timeline significantly — stock cabinets can be ordered and received in 1–2 weeks; custom cabinets can take 10–16 weeks.
- **Your budget range:** Having a clear budget ceiling helps your contractor make recommendations that are actually realistic.

### Week 2–4: Design Development

Once the scope is defined, the design work begins. This involves:
- Measuring the kitchen precisely
- Creating a layout plan
- Selecting cabinet style and finish
- Confirming appliance selections (this is critical — see below)
- Beginning countertop and tile discussions

**The appliance timing issue:** Your appliance selections need to be locked in during the design phase, not at the end of the project. Cabinet dimensions are designed around specific appliance openings. If you change your appliance selections after cabinets are ordered, you may be paying for modifications. Lock in your appliances early.

### Week 4–8: Material Selections and Ordering

This is where the lion's share of the pre-construction time goes. You'll be making decisions on:
- Cabinets (placed on order)
- Countertop material and edge profile
- Tile for backsplash
- Flooring
- Fixtures (sink, faucet, lighting)
- Hardware (cabinet pulls, hinges)

These selections take time — not because they're difficult, but because there are many options and you want to be sure before you commit. Your contractor should be guiding this process and helping you coordinate decisions so things don't fall through the cracks.

### The Permitting Step

In Shakopee and throughout the Twin Cities metro, any structural work, electrical changes, or significant plumbing work requires permits. Your contractor should submit permit applications early in the planning process — permitting in our area typically takes 1–3 weeks, and you don't want to be waiting for a permit while your cabinets are sitting in a warehouse ready to go.

## Phase 2: Pre-Construction Preparation (1–2 Weeks)

### Clearing the Kitchen

Before demo day, you'll need to:
- Clear out the entire kitchen
- Set up a temporary kitchen in another part of the house (mini fridge, countertop burner or hot plate, microwave if not already in the kitchen, and proximity to a bathroom sink)
- Plan for meals during the renovation

The temporary kitchen setup is something many homeowners don't think about until it's too late. You'll be living without a full kitchen for 6–10 weeks. Having a plan — and being realistic about how much cooking you'll want to do in a makeshift setup — will make the process much more manageable.

### Confirming Material Delivery

Before demo begins, confirm the delivery windows for your most important materials, particularly cabinets. Starting demo before cabinets are confirmed and on their way is how projects stall out in the middle.

## Phase 3: Construction (5–8 Weeks for a Typical Mid-Range Kitchen)

Here's a week-by-week look at what the construction phase typically looks like:

### Week 1: Demo and Rough-In

- Demolition of existing cabinets, countertops, flooring, and sometimes walls
- Structural work if any walls are moving
- Rough-in plumbing — moving the sink location if applicable, adding supply and drain for an island
- Rough-in electrical — new circuits, outlet repositioning, lighting rough-in
- Inspection of rough-in work (required before walls close)

This is also the week where surprises show up. Old knob-and-tube wiring, water-damaged subfloor, or an unlevel floor can add time and cost. This is why the contingency budget matters.

### Week 2: Mechanical and Prep

- HVAC adjustments if needed
- Subfloor leveling and preparation
- Drywall repair and patching after rough-in
- Any insulation changes
- Priming and prep work

### Week 3: Cabinet Installation

Cabinet day is one of the most exciting days of a kitchen remodel — the room starts looking like a kitchen for the first time. Cabinet installation for a typical kitchen takes 2–3 days, but allow a full week for this phase including adjustments, shimming, and door/drawer adjustment.

### Week 4: Countertop Template and Tile Work

- **Countertop templating:** Once cabinets are installed, the countertop fabricator comes to template (measure precisely). If you're using stone (quartz, granite, quartzite), fabrication after templating typically takes 7–14 business days.
- **Flooring installation** (if not LVP, which can go in earlier)
- **Backsplash tile prep** can begin

This is often the week with the most "hurry up and wait." Countertops can't go in until cabinets are installed. The backsplash can't be finalized until countertops are in. The best contractors are coordinating the trades to minimize gaps.

### Week 5–6: Countertops, Tile, and Finishes

- **Countertop installation** (typically 1 day once fabricated)
- **Backsplash tile installation and grouting**
- **Appliance delivery and installation** — rough-in connections are made
- **Cabinet hardware installation**
- **Trim and molding**

### Week 7–8: Fixtures, Final Details, and Punch List

- **Plumbing fixtures:** Sink and faucet installed and connected
- **Lighting fixtures:** All fixtures hung and tested
- **Final electrical connections:** Outlets, switches, under-cabinet lighting
- **Touch-up painting**
- **Final cleaning**
- **Punch list walk-through:** You walk through the project with your contractor and note anything that needs correction

## What Causes Delays (And How to Avoid Them)

### Backordered Materials

Supply chain issues have been more common in recent years, and certain cabinet lines, appliances, and specialty tiles can have extended lead times. Your contractor should be tracking all orders and flagging any delays early enough to adjust.

The safest approach: have your materials ordered and confirmed before construction begins, not after.

### Decision Fatigue and Change Orders

Changing your mind after materials are ordered is expensive. Changing your mind after the framing is done is very expensive. The best way to avoid this: take the time upfront to make decisions you're confident in. Don't order cabinets until you're sure about the layout. Don't order tile until you've lived with the sample for a week.

Changes are inevitable in remodeling — but changes that happen because the decision wasn't made clearly at the right time are avoidable.

### Scheduling Subcontractors

Your GC coordinates plumbers, electricians, counter fabricators, and tile setters. These trades have their own schedules and availability. A good contractor plans ahead and has solid relationships with reliable subs. A disorganized contractor has subs who don't show when expected, which creates cascading delays.

Ask your contractor upfront: how do you handle subcontractor scheduling? What's your process if a sub doesn't show up?

## The Total Realistic Timeline

Here's a realistic total timeline for a mid-range kitchen remodel:

- **Planning and design:** 4–8 weeks
- **Material ordering and permitting:** 3–6 weeks (overlaps with design)
- **Construction:** 5–8 weeks
- **Total from first conversation to finished kitchen:** approximately **14–20 weeks**

For a complex project with a full gut, layout changes, or custom cabinetry, allow 20–28 weeks.

This is why starting the planning process early matters. If you want a finished kitchen for Thanksgiving, start the conversation in June or July. If you want to start construction in spring, start planning in January.
