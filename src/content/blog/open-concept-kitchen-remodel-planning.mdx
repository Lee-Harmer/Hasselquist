The open concept kitchen is one of those renovation ideas that almost everyone loves  -  until they start actually planning one. Then comes the worry: Is that wall load-bearing? What happens to my outlets? Where does the range hood go if there's no wall? And of course  -  what will this actually cost?

I've helped homeowners across Shakopee, Chanhassen, Eden Prairie, and Minnetonka open up their kitchens, and the result is almost always transformative. But it requires careful planning to do it right. This guide covers everything you need to know before you swing a sledgehammer.

## Why Open Concept Is So Popular

Let's start with why people want this in the first place, because understanding the "why" helps you make better decisions about the "how."

**It changes how your family lives.** When the kitchen is open to the living and dining areas, whoever is cooking isn't isolated from the rest of the household. You can keep an eye on kids, have a conversation with guests, or watch the game without missing anything. For families with young children  -  which describes a huge percentage of homeowners in Shakopee, Prior Lake, and the surrounding suburbs  -  this is an enormous quality-of-life improvement.

**It makes spaces feel bigger.** A 1,500 square foot home with an open floor plan often feels more spacious than a 2,000 square foot home with chopped-up rooms. Visual flow and natural light distribution make a huge difference.

**It improves resale value.** Open floor plans consistently rank among the features buyers value most in the Twin Cities market. This is a renovation that appeals both to how you live in your home today and to its appeal when it's time to sell.

## The First Question: Is That Wall Load-Bearing?

This is the question everyone asks  -  and it's the most important one to answer before anything else. A load-bearing wall carries structural weight from above (whether that's another floor, a roof, or both). Removing it without proper support creates a serious safety hazard.

Here's how to approach this:

**Do not assume.** Many homeowners assume walls running parallel to the floor joists are non-load-bearing. This is often true but not always. The only way to know for certain is to have a contractor or structural engineer evaluate the wall, look at the framing in your attic or basement, and understand your home's structural system.

**Load-bearing walls require a beam.** If the wall you want to remove is load-bearing, you don't simply remove it  -  you replace it with a properly sized structural beam (LVL or steel) that transfers the load to the foundation through posts at each end. This is absolutely doable and extremely common, but it adds cost and must be done correctly with a permit.

**The beam needs to land somewhere solid.** Posts that support the beam need to land on a footing or foundation that can carry the load. In most cases, we work with the existing structure. Occasionally we need to beef up a foundation point, which can add cost.

**Budget for the unexpected.** Once we open the wall, we sometimes find old knob-and-tube wiring, plumbing runs, HVAC ducts, or other surprises. A contingency of 10–15% of your project budget is wise for any project involving wall removal.

## The Permit Reality

In Shakopee and throughout the Twin Cities metro, removing a load-bearing wall requires a building permit and typically a structural engineer's approval. I know permits sound like bureaucratic overhead, but they exist for good reason  -  and an inspector catching a problem with a beam installation before your walls are closed is far better than discovering it after the fact.

An unpermitted structural change can also create serious problems when you sell your home. I pull permits on every project that requires them, and I'd encourage you to walk away from any contractor who suggests skipping them.

## Designing Your Open Concept Kitchen

Once you've assessed the structural situation, the fun part begins: actually designing the space. Here are the key decisions you'll be making.

### Defining the Island

In most open concept kitchens, an island becomes the functional and visual centerpiece. It provides counter space, seating, storage, and creates a natural boundary between the kitchen work zone and the living/dining area without closing it off.

Key island decisions:
- **Size**  -  Allow at least 42 inches of clearance on all sides for comfortable circulation; 48 inches is better if you have the space
- **Seating overhang**  -  If you want bar seating, you need at least 12–15 inches of overhang and enough height (standard counter is 36", bar height is 42")
- **Waterfall edge or standard edge**  -  A waterfall countertop (where the stone wraps down the sides) is a popular high-end detail
- **Electrical**  -  You'll want outlets in or on the island; code requires them in islands over 12 square feet
- **Sink or cooktop**  -  Moving the sink or range into the island requires plumbing and gas/electrical runs under the floor, which adds cost but can dramatically improve functionality

### Kitchen Triangle vs. Kitchen Zones

The traditional kitchen triangle (sink, stove, refrigerator) is still relevant, but in an open concept design, most designers now think in terms of **work zones**: a prep zone, a cooking zone, a cleanup zone, and a storage zone. The goal is to minimize how many steps you take while cooking and to keep the work zones away from the main traffic path.

In an open concept layout, you want your back (and your mess) pointing away from where guests will gather. Nobody wants to chat across the kitchen island while staring at a pile of dirty dishes at the sink. Think carefully about sightlines.

### The Range Hood Problem (And Solution)

One of the most common design challenges in open concept kitchens is the range hood. If your range is moving to an island, or if a wall was removed and there's no longer a wall to mount a hood against, you need a ceiling-mounted island range hood.

Island range hoods are beautiful focal points in kitchen design, but they require:
- Duct routing through the ceiling to the exterior (not always simple)
- Adequate CFM for your cooking style
- A design that complements your kitchen aesthetic

This is entirely solvable, but it needs to be in your plan from the beginning, not an afterthought.

### Flooring Continuity

When you remove a wall and connect two previously separate rooms, you often discover that the flooring is different on each side  -  or that there's a gap where the wall was. Carrying the same flooring throughout the new unified space is usually the right design move and can actually be one of the more affordable parts of an open concept project if you're already budgeting for new floors.

LVP, hardwood, and large-format tile all work beautifully in open concept kitchen/living spaces. We have a dedicated post on [flooring choices for home renovations](/blog/best-flooring-options-home-renovation) if you want to dig deeper.

### Lighting Planning

Open concept spaces need thoughtful lighting design. You'll want distinct lighting zones that allow you to have bright task lighting in the kitchen while the living area is on a softer setting.

Plan for:
- **Recessed lights** on separate dimmer switches for kitchen and living areas
- **Pendants over the island**  -  typically 2–3 pendants spaced evenly, hanging 30–36 inches above the countertop
- **Under-cabinet lighting** in the kitchen
- **Ambient lighting** in the living/dining area (canned lights, sconces, or a statement fixture)

All of this needs to be wired  -  which is another reason why this is the right time to think about your whole electrical plan, not just the kitchen.

## What Does an Open Concept Kitchen Renovation Cost?

This varies significantly based on the scope, but here are realistic ranges for our market:

- **Wall removal only (non-load-bearing):** $1,500–$4,000
- **Load-bearing wall removal with beam:** $5,000–$15,000 depending on span and complexity
- **Full open concept kitchen remodel** (wall removal + kitchen renovation): $45,000–$90,000+

The wall removal and structural work is typically 10–20% of the total project cost. The majority of the budget goes to the kitchen itself  -  cabinetry, countertops, appliances, flooring, and lighting.

## The Timeline

An open concept kitchen remodel is not a weekend project. Depending on complexity, expect:

- Planning and design: 2–4 weeks
- Permitting: 1–3 weeks (varies by municipality)
- Demo and structural work: 1–2 weeks
- Rough-in (plumbing, electrical, HVAC): 1–2 weeks
- Cabinetry, countertops, finishes: 3–5 weeks
- Punch list and final touches: 1 week

Total: approximately 8–14 weeks from signed contract to finished project. We cover timeline planning in detail in our post on [how to plan a kitchen remodel timeline](/blog/how-to-plan-kitchen-remodel-timeline).
