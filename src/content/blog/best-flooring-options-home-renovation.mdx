Flooring is one of the most impactful decisions in any home renovation  -  and also one of the most confusing. Walk into any flooring showroom and you're confronted with hundreds of options across a dozen categories, all with their own pros, cons, and price points. The salesperson wants to sell you something. The internet gives you conflicting advice. And you're just trying to figure out what's actually going to look good and hold up in your home.

I've installed flooring in hundreds of homes across Shakopee, Eden Prairie, Bloomington, and the broader Twin Cities area. Here's my honest, practical breakdown of the options  -  what each one is actually good for, where it falls short, and what you should expect to pay.

## Hardwood Flooring

Real hardwood is the gold standard of flooring for a reason: it's beautiful, it adds genuine value to your home, and it can be refinished multiple times over its lifetime. A well-maintained hardwood floor can last 50–100 years. I've sanded and refinished floors in homes throughout Edina and Minnetonka that were 60+ years old and looked like new after the work.

**Best for:** Living rooms, dining rooms, hallways, bedrooms, and open areas where you want lasting beauty and the ability to refinish.

**Not ideal for:** High-moisture areas (bathrooms, laundry rooms), below-grade installation (basements), or homes with large dogs who will scratch the finish constantly.

### Species to Consider

- **Red Oak**  -  The most common species in Minnesota homes; warm, durable, takes stain well
- **White Oak**  -  Slightly harder than red oak, with a more subtle grain; very popular right now in contemporary and transitional designs
- **Maple**  -  Very hard, light color, works beautifully in modern kitchens and great rooms
- **Hickory**  -  Extremely hard and rustic-looking; great for high-traffic areas

### Cost

Solid hardwood typically runs $6–$12 per square foot for materials, plus $4–$8 per square foot for installation. Engineered hardwood (real wood veneer over a plywood core) is more stable across seasons and slightly less expensive at $4–$10 per square foot for materials.

In Minnesota's climate with its significant humidity swings, engineered hardwood is often the smarter choice  -  it moves less with seasonal changes and can be installed on or below grade.

## Luxury Vinyl Plank (LVP)

LVP has genuinely transformed the flooring industry over the past decade, and for good reason. Today's LVP products look remarkably close to real wood, are completely waterproof, and can withstand the kind of punishment that families with kids and pets dish out.

**Best for:** Kitchens, basements, bathrooms, mudrooms, and really anywhere you want a wood look with maximum durability. Also excellent for whole-home installations where you want the same floor throughout for visual continuity.

**Not ideal for:** If you're trying to maximize resale value in a high-end home where buyers expect hardwood, LVP in the main living areas may read as a compromise. In entry-level and mid-range homes, this distinction matters much less.

### What to Look For in LVP

The most important spec is the **wear layer thickness**. This is the protective layer on top of the vinyl core.

- **6 mil wear layer:** Budget option, fine for low-traffic areas
- **12 mil:** Good for residential use in most rooms
- **20 mil+:** Commercial-grade; excellent for high-traffic residential use

I consistently recommend 12 mil minimum for any full home installation. If you have large dogs or anticipate heavy use, go to 20 mil.

**Core type** also matters. "WPC" (wood-plastic composite) cores are thicker, cushier underfoot, and more forgiving over slightly uneven subfloors. "SPC" (stone-plastic composite) cores are thinner, denser, and more dimensionally stable  -  often the better choice in Minnesota where basement subfloors may have minor imperfections.

### Cost

Quality LVP runs $2.50–$5.50 per square foot for materials, plus $2–$4 per square foot for installation. This is significantly less expensive than hardwood for both materials and labor, making it an excellent choice for maximizing your renovation budget.

## Tile

Ceramic and porcelain tile is essential in bathrooms and laundry rooms, and it's a strong choice for kitchens, mudrooms, and entryways. It's completely impervious to water, extremely durable, and available in an enormous range of looks  -  including very convincing wood-look and stone-look options.

**Best for:** Bathrooms, laundry rooms, mudrooms, entryways, kitchens. Also the right choice for basement floors in areas that might see occasional moisture.

**Not ideal for:** Living rooms and bedrooms (hard and cold underfoot unless you add radiant heat), and it's unforgiving if you drop dishes or glasses.

### Porcelain vs. Ceramic

Both are kiln-fired clay products. Porcelain is fired at higher temperatures, making it denser, less porous, and more durable. For most floor applications, porcelain is the better choice. Ceramic tile is fine for walls and light-use areas.

For wet areas in Minnesota, look for tile with a **COF (coefficient of friction) rating of 0.42 or higher** for floors  -  this indicates adequate slip resistance.

### Large Format Tile

As I mentioned in the bathroom trends post, large-format tile (12x24, 24x24, or larger) is dominant right now. It creates fewer grout lines, looks cleaner, and makes spaces feel larger. The tradeoff is that installation is more labor-intensive  -  large tiles are less forgiving on subfloors that aren't perfectly level, and they require more precision to set.

### Cost

Standard ceramic or porcelain tile runs $1.50–$6 per square foot for materials. Premium large-format tile, natural stone, or designer series can run $8–$20+ per square foot. Installation costs are typically $8–$15 per square foot, making tile one of the higher labor-cost flooring options.

## Carpet

Carpet has taken a backlash in recent years, but it remains the right choice for certain spaces  -  especially bedrooms and basement family rooms where warmth and softness underfoot matter.

**Best for:** Master bedrooms, children's bedrooms, basement family rooms, and stairs.

**Not ideal for:** Kitchens, bathrooms, mudrooms, or any high-moisture area. And if you have severe allergies, carpet's tendency to trap dust and pet dander is a real consideration.

### What Matters in Carpet Quality

**Fiber type:** Nylon is the most durable and the standard recommendation for high-traffic areas. Polyester is softer and more stain-resistant but less durable. Wool is luxurious and natural but expensive.

**Density and pile weight:** Higher face weight (measured in ounces per square yard) means more fiber, which means more durability and better feel underfoot. For bedrooms, 40–50 oz is a comfortable range. For stairs and high-traffic areas, 50+ oz is better.

**The pad matters:** A quality 8 lb density pad under your carpet makes a significant difference in feel, durability, and sound insulation. Don't cheap out on the pad.

### Cost

Carpet ranges enormously by quality, from $1.50 to $8+ per square foot for materials. Installation typically runs $1–$2 per square foot, making carpet generally the most affordable flooring option overall.

## Making the Right Choice Room by Room

Here's a quick reference for typical Minnesota homes:

| Room | First Choice | Good Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| Main living area | Hardwood or engineered hardwood | Quality LVP |
| Kitchen | LVP or large-format tile | Hardwood (with good sealing) |
| Master bathroom | Porcelain tile | N/A |
| Guest bath | Porcelain tile | N/A |
| Master bedroom | Carpet or hardwood | LVP |
| Kids' bedrooms | Carpet | LVP |
| Basement | LVP | Tile in wet areas |
| Mudroom | Tile | LVP |
| Stairs | Hardwood or carpet | LVP (with nosing) |

## A Note on Minnesota's Climate

Our climate puts specific demands on flooring. Humidity in Minnesota swings significantly from summer (when it can be quite humid) to winter (when central heating dries indoor air dramatically). These swings cause natural wood to expand and contract seasonally.

This is manageable with proper installation (adequate expansion gaps, acclimation of materials before installation) but it does mean that solid hardwood in a below-grade basement is genuinely a bad idea  -  LVP or tile is the right call there.
